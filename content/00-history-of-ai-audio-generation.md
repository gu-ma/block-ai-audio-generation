# 00 — History of AI Audio Generation

This timeline offers a creative overview of how AI audio tools evolved, and why current workflows feel so fast, expressive, and collaborative.

## 1) Rule-based and algorithmic composition (1950s–1970s)

- Early computer music systems used explicit rules, probability, and formal procedures.
- Artists could generate patterns, harmonies, and rhythms, but control was manual, technical, and often mediated by code.
- Main idea: **machines as composition assistants**, not full creative partners.

**Example art projects**

- [_ILLIAC Suite_ (Lejaren Hiller & Leonard Isaacson, 1957)](https://distributedmuseum.illinois.edu/exhibit/illiac-suite/) — often cited as the first string quartet composed with the help of a computer.
- [Iannis Xenakis — _ST/10_ (1962)](https://www.iannis-xenakis.org/en/st-10/) — a landmark in stochastic and algorithmic composition.

## 2) Statistical + symbolic music systems (1960s–2000s)

- Markov chains, grammars, and symbolic models generated MIDI-like or score-like sequences.
- These systems were useful for structured continuation: chord progressions, melody generation, and style imitation.
- Main limitation: they could model **musical structure** better than **musical sound**.

**Example art projects**

- [David Cope — _Experiments in Musical Intelligence (EMI)_](https://www.areditions.com/cope-experiments-in-musical-intelligence-2nd-ed-das012.html) — a major symbolic AI system for generating music in learned stylistic idioms.
- [Xenakis’s UPIC system](https://www.iannis-xenakis.org/en/dictionary-upic/) — a composition interface where drawing could become sound, expanding algorithmic composition into graphical interaction.

## 3) Deep learning for audio (2010s)

- Neural audio synthesis began scaling with larger datasets and more compute.
- Waveform models could generate much more realistic speech and audio directly from samples rather than only symbolic descriptions.
- Main shift: from symbolic control toward **high-fidelity waveform modeling**.

**Example projects**

- [WaveNet (DeepMind)](https://deepmind.google/research/wavenet/) — a breakthrough model for raw audio generation that strongly influenced later speech and music synthesis.
- [NSynth (Google Magenta)](https://magenta.withgoogle.com/nsynth) — a neural synthesis project that let musicians interpolate between instrument timbres in new ways.

## 4) Neural TTS and voice personalization

- End-to-end TTS pipelines improved prosody, naturalness, and expressive timing.
- Speaker embeddings made it possible to adapt systems to different vocal identities and styles.
- Main shift: voice became **programmable** — not just readable, but shapeable.

**Example art projects**

- [Holly Herndon’s _PROTO_ / Spawn project](https://www.nvidia.com/en-us/research/ai-art-gallery/artists/holly-herndon/) — an influential example of AI voice systems used as artistic collaborators rather than invisible tools.
- [“Godmother” by Holly Herndon & Jlin (feat. Spawn)](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=sc9OjL6Mjqo) — a visible cultural example of machine-learned vocal identity entering music practice.

## 5) Foundation models for music and audio (2020s)

- Transformer, autoregressive, and diffusion-style systems expanded from speech to music and environmental audio.
- Prompt-based generation made audio creation accessible to non-specialists.
- Main shift: creators moved from “model building” to **prompt + iteration workflows**.

**Example art projects**

- [Jukebox (OpenAI)](https://openai.com/index/jukebox/) — a widely cited raw-audio music model showing genre, style, and lyric-conditioned generation.
- [Dadabots](https://www.dadabots.com/) — an artist-research duo using neural networks for continuous music generation and livestreamed machine-made sound.
- [AIVA](https://www.aiva.ai/) — an example of AI-assisted composition becoming part of everyday creative tooling.

## 6) Current era: multimodal and production pipelines

- Audio models now connect with text, image, video, and editing workflows.
- Generation, editing, stem control, style transfer, and post-processing are increasingly integrated.
- Main creative challenge is less “can we generate?” and more **how to direct, curate, and justify choices**.

**Example art projects**

- [Refik Anadol — _Echoes of the Earth: Living Archive_](https://refikanadol.com/works/echoes-of-the-earth-living-archive/) — an example of AI art moving into large-scale multimodal installation practice.
- [Google’s NSynth Sound Maker](https://experiments.withgoogle.com/sound-maker) — an accessible example of AI audio generation becoming part of interactive creative interfaces.

## Practical takeaway for studio

History shows a steady movement from rigid control to generative co-creation. Your creative value now comes from:

- framing intent,
- directing model behavior,
- selecting and refining outputs,
- connecting sound to wider visual or narrative systems,
- documenting ethics, authorship, and provenance.

In other words: the tool became faster, but the creative responsibility became deeper.
